Understanding "Have" vs "Have Got"

The verbs "have" and "have got" are commonly used in English to express possession, relationships, illnesses, and characteristics. Although they can often be used interchangeably, there are some differences in their usage and form.

Usage of "Have"

"Have" is used in both American and British English. It is slightly more formal and can be used in a variety of contexts:

Example:

I have a new car.

She has two brothers.

They have an important meeting today.

Usage of "Have Got"

"Have got" is more commonly used in British English and in informal contexts. It carries the same meaning as "have" but is often used when speaking casually:

Example:

I have got a new car.

She has got two brothers.

They have got an important meeting today.

Forming Questions

To form questions, "have" and "have got" are used differently:

  • With "Have": Do you have a pen?
  • With "Have Got": Have you got a pen?

Negatives

Negative sentences also have slight differences in form:

  • With "Have": I don't have any money.
  • With "Have Got": I haven't got any money.
